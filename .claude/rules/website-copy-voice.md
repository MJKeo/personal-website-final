# Rule: Website Copy Voice (Michael's professional register)

Applies to **all user-facing text on the website**: project and experience
write-ups, taglines, summaries, and any page copy that a visitor reads. (Internal
docs like `ARCHITECTURE.md`/`TODOs.md` are exempt.)

The base voice is still **Michael's**, per
`personal_context/my_writing_style.md`: problem-first, reasoning always exposed,
opinionated and willing to commit to a view, grounded in concrete examples,
first-person and warm, with "ex." for examples, parentheticals, and the
"not just X, it's Y" corrective reframe. Keep all of that.

**This rule is a professional overlay on top of that style guide, and it OVERRIDES
the casual parts of the guide for site copy.** The site is a hiring asset, so the
register is "Michael explaining his work confidently to a smart hiring engineer,"
not "Michael thinking out loud to a friend."

## Hard rules (never violate on site copy)

1. **Never undermine, downplay, or self-deprecate about his own work.** Do not
   write that something he built was easy, trivial, simple, "not much," or that
   "most of it was straightforward," or anything that makes his contribution sound
   lesser. Honest scoping is fine (ex. "this never shipped", "it was still early"),
   but never frame the *difficulty or value of what he made* as small. When in
   doubt, describe what made it hard or what it taught him, not how easy it was.
   - Banned example (real, from a draft): *"What stuck with me wasn't really any of
     the code, cus honestly most of it was straightforward."* This both undermines
     the work and uses "cus". Never do either.
2. **Never use "cus" (or "cuz").** Also drop the other casual slang spellings for
   site copy: "gonna", "gotta", "wanna", "lowkey", "y'know". Write the full words.
3. **Keep ALL-CAPS emphasis rare** (prefer none). Don't lean on it to carry a point.
4. **Cut filler and diminutive-casual words:** "basically", "a bunch of", "stuff",
   "annoying", "pretty" (as in "pretty simple"), "a heck of a lot", and similar.
5. **Carry over the base guide's hard don'ts:** no em dashes ever, no "e.g."/"i.e."
   (use "ex."), no corporate fluff ("leverage", "utilize", "seamless",
   "cutting-edge"), no feature-first openings, no invented numbers.

## Litmus

If a passage sounds like Michael, leads with the problem, exposes the reasoning,
commits to an opinion, uses a concrete "ex.", has zero em dashes, contains **no
"cus" or slang**, and **never diminishes what he built**, it passes. If it reads
casual to the point of undercutting him, or slips in "cus", it fails.
